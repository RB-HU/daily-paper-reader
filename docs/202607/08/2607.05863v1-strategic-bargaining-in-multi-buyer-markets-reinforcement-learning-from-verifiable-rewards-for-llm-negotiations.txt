Negotiation is a fundamental strategic interaction in management science, characterized by agents attempt-ing to reach agreements while protecting private information, such as reservation costs and hidden valuations. A prevalent yet complex scenario involves a single seller negotiating concurrently with multiple buyers, each possessing heterogeneous, private budgets. In such settings, constrained by a limited number of communi-cation turns, the seller must balance exploring the broader market to discover the highest valuation with concentrating sufficient turns on a single target buyer to secure the best possible outcome. Our analysis reveals a significant gap in standard Large Language Models (LLMs): while these models are linguistically proficient, they fail to act as effective economic decision-makers. Specifically, they exhibit a failure to explore the buyer pool, often fixating on the current highest bid rather than strategically investigating the market to discover latent high valuations. In this paper, we propose a specialized training recipe using Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). By anchoring the reward function to objective economic outcomes, the strategic balance between market discovery and surplus extraction emerges natively through the learning process. Our results demonstrate that the trained seller undergoes a multi-stage strategic evolution, learning to leverage price anchoring and strategic probing to identify more profitable counterparties. The agent extracts substantially higher surplus than frontier models by both improving its persuasive bargaining skills and consistently closing deals with high-value buyers. Finally, we show that our seller strategies generalize robustly to unseen buyer negotiation styles and budget distributions. 

Key words : LLM agents; reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards; negotiation; multi-agent bargaining; surplus extraction

## 1. Introduction 

Negotiation is a foundational process in management science and operations research, serving as the primary mechanism for value discovery and resource allocation in environments where prices are not static (Simchi-Levi et al. 2005, Backus et al. 2020). In fields ranging from procurement and supply chain contracting to dispute resolution and online marketplaces, agents must strategically interact to coordinate and realize gains from trade (Cachon and Lariviere 2001, Desai and Purohit 2004). Within management science literature, the analysis of these strategic interactions focuses heavily on aligning decentralized incentives and resolving information gaps between trading partners (Nagarajan and Soˇ si´ c 2008, Lovejoy 2010). A central challenge in any negotiation is that agents possess private information—specifically, a seller has a minimum cost they are willing to accept, and a buyer has a maximum budget they are willing to pay—which remains hidden from their counterpart (Chatterjee and Samuelson 1983). To capture meaningful value under these asymmetric conditions, a seller must maintain strict strategic discipline. This requires simultaneously enforcing a private cost boundary to prevent loss-making transactions, reading buyer signals to extract maximum market surplus, and effectively coordinating with the buyer to establish a common price point and close the deal. While classical literature often focuses on bilateral (one-on-one) trade (Nash et al. 1950, Rubinstein 1982, Chatterjee and Samuelson 1983), a highly prevalent scenario in modern management is concurrent negotiation, where a single focal seller interacts simultaneously with multiple independent buyers across separate, sealed channels (Backus et al. 2020). This parallel structure is central to digital marketplaces like eBay and business-to-business procurement platforms, where a seller allocates an available asset or supply contract by negotiating across a pool of potential buyers (Backus et al. 2020). Crucially, because the seller operates under a limited communication capacity across these parallel channels, it faces a fundamental trade-off between exploration and exploitation. The seller cannot afford extended bargaining cycles with every participant; instead, it must balance broad market screening to identify the counterparty with the highest hidden valuation against concentrated, single-buyer bargaining to maximize surplus extraction. When deploying autonomous or automated agents in these settings to act as direct representatives of the firm, failing to locate the optimal partner or settling for thin margins with an early bidder results in an immediate loss of organizational value. To manage these concurrent negotiations at scale, firms are actively deploying Large Language Models (LLMs) as autonomous bargaining agents. In digital platforms like Facebook Marketplace, automated tools assist users by managing simultaneous price inquiries and drafting real-time offers across independent buyer channels (Meta 2026). Similarly, in enterprise procurement, retail leaders like Walmart utilize automated negotiation systems to execute binding contracts with thousands of Liu et al.: LLM Negotiation with Verifiable Rewards 3

tail-end suppliers, bypassing the capacity constraints of human purchasing teams (Van Hoek et al. 2022). However, these current implementations operate primarily as reactive text filters designed to minimize conversational friction (Meta 2026), rather than strategic revenue management systems (Gallego and Hu 2014). Faced with a limited communication budget, the agent’s primary task is not conversational fluency, but using the dialogue to screen hidden buyer valuations and protect profit margins by allocating communication efforts to the highest-value counterparties. Our analysis reveals a critical misalignment between this required strategic discipline and the operational behavior of generalist frontier models. While standard LLMs excel at maintaining conversational fluency, they typically exhibit an agreeableness bias, maximizing total deal volume at the expense of overall economic surplus (Ouyang et al. 2022, Perez et al. 2023, Sharma et al. 2024). Because these models are optimized via reinforcement learning from human feedback for politeness and cooperation, they are prone to making suboptimal concessions rather than investigating the buyer pool to discover latent high valuations. Consequently, this bias turns their conversational fluency into a financial liability, preventing the agent from maximizing captured surplus. This paper addresses these limitations. We make three primary contributions: 1. A concurrent negotiation framework for evaluating exploration-exploitation trade-offs (Section 3). We establish a structured environment where a seller negotiates concurrently against multiple buyers with diverse budgets through private, one-on-one communication channels. This setting allows us to measure an agent’s ability to balance the strategic discovery of high-budget buyers with focused bargaining to extract surplus under limited communication turns. 2. A behavioral analysis identifying the exploration failure in frontier LLMs (Sec-tion 5). We demonstrate that even state-of-the-art general-purpose models, including those with specialized reasoning capabilities, fail to maximize surplus in multi-buyer settings. Our results reveal a systematic tendency to settle for suboptimal deals by fixating on early high bids rather than investigating the broader buyer pool to identify buyers with higher private budgets. 3. A specialized training recipe for strategic agents using Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (Section 4). We demonstrate that Reinforcement Learning from Veri-fiable Rewards (RLVR) can effectively bridge the gap between conversational fluency and strategic utility. Our formulation utilizes objective economic outcomes—specifically deal surplus—and strict instruction-following protocols to guide the learning process. Our results show that this approach enables the emergence of proactive market discovery mechanisms, such as diagnostic probing and price anchoring, allowing agents to identify profitable partners even when facing unfamiliar buyer behaviors and unseen budget distributions. ## 2. Related Work 

We organize the relevant literature into the following streams: negotiation in management science, the evaluation of LLMs as strategic agents, automated negotiation systems, reinforcement learning for language models, and the foundations of classical bargaining theory. 

2.1. Negotiation in Management Science and Operations 

Negotiation serves as a fundamental mechanism for price discovery and supply chain coordination within management science, particularly when firms lack direct visibility into a partner’s private costs or reservation thresholds. Research in management science extensively evaluates these bargaining dynamics to optimize incentive alignment in procurement and capacity contracting under asymmetric information. Traditional literature in management science typically addresses these information gaps using screening menus, strategic information sharing frameworks, or decentralized networks to reallocate bargaining power (Cachon and Lariviere 2001, Lu et al. 2006, Nagarajan and Soˇ si´ c2008, Lovejoy 2010). Additionally, when inventory is limited and buyers are strategic, bargaining interfaces deeply with classical capacity contracting and revenue management, as sellers must anticipate how partner valuations and cost horizons shift over time (Aviv and Pazgal 2008, Su 2010); this operational tension requires tailored contracts that adjust payment timing to balance inventory choices with the financial costs faced by each stage of the supply chain (Tong et al. 2020). In digital platforms and retail operations, the market landscape has rapidly shifted from static posted pricing toward highly responsive, competitive dynamic pricing frameworks (Gallego and Hu 2014, Wang and Hu 2014, Ban and Keskin 2021) alongside multi-round bilateral negotiations and sequential clearing processes (Desai and Purohit 2004, Backus et al. 2020). This transition fundamentally alters platform resource allocation, intertemporal price discrimination, and the strategic distribution of market surplus. Large-scale empirical marketplace data confirms that sequential counter-offers account for a significant portion of online transactions, establishing multi-turn dialog as a major channel for price discovery (Backus et al. 2020, Bergemann et al. 2026). Our work addresses a critical intersection of these fields by modeling a concurrent market structure where a single seller must allocate a limited number of communication turns across multiple separate, private negotiation channels. This simultaneous challenge aligns with revenue management streams demonstrating that firms maximize performance by executing learning and operational actions Liu et al.: LLM Negotiation with Verifiable Rewards 5

concurrently rather than sequentially (Agrawal et al. 2014, Wang et al. 2014). Under this formulation, gathering market data through strategic interactions serves as a core requirement for protecting reservation costs and ensuring overall profitability (Guo 2023). To navigate this high-dimensional environment without relying on restrictive structural assumptions, our approach builds upon the growing paradigm of integrating data-driven prescriptive analytics and machine learning directly into operational decision rules (Ban and Rudin 2019). 

2.2. LLMs as Negotiating and Strategic Agents 

Recent work has increasingly utilized Large Language Models (LLMs) as autonomous agents in goal-directed settings, ranging from tool-assisted reasoning to complex decision-making tasks (Yao et al. 2022, Liu et al. 2024, Wang et al. 2024a, Zeng et al. 2024). However, systematic evaluations have revealed significant behavioral shortcomings in their strategic reasoning and operational logic. Xia et al. (2024) find that frontier models frequently violate principles of negotiation rationality and are susceptible to adversarial pressure, while Davidson et al. (2024) document a failure to maintain consistent strategic goals in multi-turn interactions. Furthermore, general-purpose LLMs often struggle to manage the tension between cooperation and competition, a core requirement of effective bargaining (Fu et al. 2023). To evaluate these capabilities, Bianchi et al. (2024) establish a platform to analyze how general-purpose models navigate bargaining dynamics. Subsequently, Bergemann et al. (2026) and Liu et al. (2026) demonstrate that specialized reinforcement learning can enhance the strategic competence of agents in bilateral (one-on-one) trade. While these existing approaches are restricted to bilateral negotiation settings, we develop a reinforcement learning method for concurrent multi-buyer markets. This allows us to address a systematic market exploration failure where frontier models fixate on immediate bids rather than investigating alternative candidates. Unlike purely one-on-one training, our method enables the agent to resolve the broader tension between market discovery and value extraction. 

2.3. Automated Negotiation and Dialogue Systems 

The development of automated bargaining agents predates the current LLM era, typically operating in structured, language-free environments. Early systems utilized game-theoretic heuristics or reinforcement learning trained on historical transaction data (Buettner 1997, Jennings et al. 2001, Kraus 2001, Green and Plunkett 2022). More recent efforts have integrated natural language, though often relying on modular architectures that decouple numerical pricing strategies from text generation (Lewis et al. 2017, He et al. 2018, Yarats and Lewis 2018). While LLMs have removed these linguistic bottlenecks, they have introduced a new need for specialized training to ensure strategic effectiveness (Chawla et al. 2023) and high-level goal 6 Liu et al.: LLM Negotiation with Verifiable Rewards 

consistency in the face of counterparty pressure (Xia et al. 2024). Efforts to improve LLM performance include prompt-based modular systems that utilize game-theoretic feedback (Fu et al. 2023) and specialized prompting frameworks designed to manage competitive and cooperative bargaining logic (Abdelnabi et al. 2024). Other approaches rely on supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning using synthetic negotiation datasets to improve strategic consistency (Lewis et al. 2017, Chawla et al. 2023). We build upon this lineage by providing a unified reinforcement learning (RL) pipeline that optimizes both persuasive communication and high-level market search, moving beyond modular designs toward an end-to-end strategic agent. 

2.4. Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards 

The adaptation of general-purpose Large Language Models (LLMs) for specialized domain tasks represents a central focus in post-training development. While standard approaches rely on Rein-forcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) to refine conversational outputs (Ziegler et al. 2019, Ouyang et al. 2022), such subjective preference signals are often a poor proxy for objective economic utility. Human labelers tend to reward linguistic agreeableness and superficial helpfulness over strategic rigor (Perez et al. 2023, Shah et al. 2026), creating a misalignment where models prioritize conversational fluency over effective strategic execution (Casper et al. 2023). To mitigate these biases and align model actions with concrete strategic goals, recent research has shifted toward Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) (Shao et al. 2024, Guo et al. 2025). Under this methodology, performance is evaluated programmatically through objective, quantifiable metrics, ensuring that the feedback loop remains anchored to measurable outcomes rather than subjective human judgment (Guo et al. 2025). This approach has proven effective in improving the reasoning capabilities of agents in domains such as mathematics and code generation (Cobbe et al. 2021, Ma et al. 2023, Wang et al. 2024b, Shao et al. 2024), where objective rewards—such as mathematical correctness or unit-test suc-cess—incentivize the discovery of optimal trajectories that human supervisors might not anticipate (Guo et al. 2025). We utilize this Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) frame-work to address concurrent negotiation in multi-buyer markets, where deal profit serves as a verifiable objective to guide both bargaining skills and the strategic discovery of latent market information. By tying optimization to verifiable economic outcomes, we incentivize the emergence of proactive exploration mechanisms—such as diagnostic probing and price anchoring—necessary for identifying the highest-budget buyer and acquiring high deal surplus in information-constrained environments. 2.5. Foundations of Classical Bargaining Theory 

Bargaining theory establishes the foundation for bilateral trade under asymmetric information, building on the formulations of Harsanyi (1967), Nash et al. (1950), St˚ ahl (1972), Rubinstein (1982), and Chatterjee and Samuelson (1983). A critical baseline for this domain is provided by Myerson and Satterthwaite (1983), who define the fundamental limits of allocative efficiency under private information. Subsequent game-theoretic work expanded these formulations to sequential settings with dynamic information revelation (Fudenberg and Tirole 1983, Sobel and Takahashi 1983, Gul et al. 1986). While these classical models derive analytical equilibrium conditions for bilateral trade, their strategies are restricted to numerical pricing choices and typically require the seller to know the prior distribution of market valuations. In contrast, our work targets a concurrent multi-buyer setting using a reinforcement learning approach where the agent dynamically learns to negotiate via natural language dialogue without explicit access to these market priors. 

## 3. Problem Formulation and RL Framework 

We formalize seller negotiation as a decision problem in which the seller is the only learning agent. The environment consists of N heterogeneous buyers and a system of private, one-on-one communication channels that ensures information remains hidden between participants. The seller holds a private reservation cost C and is matched against N buyers indexed i ∈ { 1, . . . , N }, each carrying a private budget Bi drawn from a distribution. All buyers see the same product, which carries a public list price Plist . But each buyer’s conversation is sealed: a buyer observes only the seller’s messages directed to that buyer, and never the other N − 1 dialogues. The seller must allocate a finite total of T messages across the N buyer slots subject to a per-buyer cap K with a total limit T < N K . Thus, the seller cannot exhaust every conversation and is forced to choose where to spend attention.

Consequently, walking away is preferred to closing at a loss with a negative reward, but it is dispreferred compared to capturing a positive surplus. This hierarchy provides the agent with a clear directional signal to both expand buyer engagement and negotiate for higher deal prices. 

Instruction violation. A reward of −1 is assigned whenever the seller violates the constraints provided in its system instructions. 3.3. Environment and Agent Specifications 

To ensure the negotiation environment is strategically rigorous, we define the following specifications for the buyers and the information environment used during training (Section 4) and in-distribution evaluation (Section 5). 

Buyer Agent Model. The N = 3 buyer slots in each episode are comprised of a trained and frozen Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 model (Qwen Team 2025). Specifically, we utilize a buyer checkpoint that was previously trained via RLVR on a bilateral (one-on-one) negotiation task from Liu et al. (2026). This ensures the seller is matched against a strong opponent that has already learned to protect its own surplus and walk away from poor deals, rather than a naive or overly agreeable baseline buyer model. 

First-Offer Regulation. To prevent the seller from identifying high-budget buyers immediately in Turn-1, we implement a first-offer regulator . In the default environment, every buyer’s opening offer is sampled from a range of [0 .25 , 0.30] · Plist , regardless of that buyer’s actual private budget. This regulation ensures that the initial “signals” from the buyers are less informative, forcing the seller to use its limited communication turns to probe for latent budget information through multi-turn dialogue. 

Budget Distribution. During training and in-distribution testing, we utilize a rank-ordered triple of budget intervals: {[0 .40 , 0.55] , [0 .65 , 0.80] , [1 .00 , 1.15] }. These intervals are assigned to the three buyer slots via a uniform permutation at the start of each episode. This setup establishes diversity in buyer valuations, ensuring that the pool always contains a high-, medium-, and low-valuation participant. Meanwhile, to maintain asymmetric information, the buyers’ specific budgets are kept hidden from the seller.

## 7. Discussion and Strategic Implications 

The results presented in Section 5 demonstrate that general-purpose Large Language Models, despite their massive scale, lack the inherent strategic discipline required to navigate concurrent multi-buyer negotiation settings. While frontier models often exhibit high “agreeableness,” this trait becomes a liability in a competitive bargaining setting where identifying the optimal counterparty is a prerequisite for surplus maximization. The behavioral evolution observed during training, together with the performance results from our benchmarking, suggests several key insights into the development of autonomous economic agents. 

Verifiable Rewards vs. Human Preferences. A central finding of this work is that standard instruction tuning and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al. 2019, Ouyang et al. 2022) are insufficient for enforcing hard economic constraints. As seen in Table 1, even thinking-mode models with a trillion parameters routinely violate the fundamental principle of 

individual rationality by selling below cost. This failure stems from the misalignment between human preference optimization—which rewards helpfulness and stylistic consistency—and the objective requirements of a verifiable economic environment. By training via RLVR, we provide the agent with a reward signal directly tied to profitability and constraint satisfaction. This creates an agent that treats economic floors not as suggestions in a prompt, but as invariant rules of the environment. 

Bridging the “Satisficing” Gap. The behavioral decomposition in Section 6.3 reveals a specific cognitive failure in baseline models: they are “satisficers” rather than optimizers in information-constrained environments (Simon 1955). When confronted with multiple buyers, baselines tend to settle for the first “good enough” offer (typically Rank-2), failing to explore the pool for higher-value opportunities. Our agent’s emergent strategy of diagnostic probing represents a significant shift in strategic logic. The agent learns to value information acquisition as a means to future reward, 26 Liu et al.: LLM Negotiation with Verifiable Rewards 

spending early turns anchoring at the list price to explore the buyer pool. This behavior mirrors classical search theory in economics, where the seller must balance the cost of further search (consuming communication turns) against the expected gain of finding a higher valuation (Diamond 1971, Bakos 1997). 

Strategic Efficiency over Parameter Scale. An evaluation of the reward metrics reveals a distinct performance gap between the 30B agent trained under our proposed framework and frontier architectures with advanced reasoning capabilities. While gpt-5.1-high-reasoning achieved the highest baseline performance with a mean reward of +0 .123, the agent optimized via our proposed Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) framework reached a mean reward of +0 .580. This outcome indicates that for highly structured strategic tasks such as concurrent negotiation, the proposed framework can be a more effective driver of utility than general-purpose scaling or test-time reasoning mechanisms. For operations management applications, these findings suggest that smaller, task-specific models can successfully execute specialized procurement or supply-chain roles, provided they are trained directly on objective economic outcomes. 

Robustness to Changes in Market Transparency. The OOD evaluation in Section 6.1 serves as a key test of whether our agent merely memorized specific training patterns or acquired general negotiation strategies. In this setting, the environment became more transparent because opening offers directly revealed buyer budgets, effectively providing baseline models with a more direct signal of the highest-value buyer. Furthermore, because this evaluation tests the seller against a diverse mix of buyer models, our agent cannot succeed by simply relying on specific opening patterns it encountered during training. While these environmental shifts narrowed the performance gap, our agent’s continued lead demonstrates the durable value of active market screening. Unlike the baselines that react immediately to high opening bids, our agent consistently explores alternative buyer channels. This thorough checking protects the agent from being misled by a high opening bid from a lower-value buyer, showing that the learned strategy safely navigates altered market dynamics by verifying signals rather than blindly trusting the buyers. 

Managerial Implications and Business Applications. The findings of this work offer a scalable blueprint for deploying autonomous agents in practical business environments where strategic discipline is paramount. Our results demonstrate that the standard “helpfulness” and “agreeableness” developed during general LLM training are significant liabilities in management settings. These models act as passive signal-followers, failing to adhere to economic constraints and settling for suboptimal surplus. However, the RLVR recipe introduced in this paper—linking objective rewards to strategic outcomes and constraint satisfaction—provides a path toward creating agents that prioritize utility over politeness. Liu et al.: LLM Negotiation with Verifiable Rewards 27 

The most immediate application lies in high-volume, information-asymmetric marketplaces such as eBay or Craigslist. In these settings, a seller often manages multiple concurrent inquiries for a single item. A general-purpose agent would be susceptible to early “low-ball” offers or high-pressure tactics; in contrast, an agent trained via our framework would utilize diagnostic probing to identify the highest-valuation buyer while strictly respecting the seller’s cost floor. Beyond simple trade, this methodology generalizes to more complex interactions such as automated business-to-business (B2B) contract negotiation or job offer management. In these domains, the interaction is defined by hard constraints (e.g., legal clauses, non-compete terms, or budget caps) and high-level business goals. By substituting subjective human preference models with verifiable economic outcomes—such as contract completion value or total compensation—firms can deploy agents capable of maintaining high-level goal consistency. This shift from agreeable AI to strategic AI helps autonomous negotiators better serve the organizations they represent by pursuing clear business objectives in competitive environments. 

Future Work. While this work focuses on a single seller against multiple buyers, real-world markets often involve double-sided competition. Future research could explore the co-evolution of both buyers and sellers in a concurrent setting to identify whether stable equilibria emerge or if cycles of predatory anchoring dominate the dialogue. Additionally, incorporating multi-product bundles or multi-attribute utility functions (e.g., delivery speed, warranty terms) would further test the limits of verifiable rewards in complex, high-dimensional bargaining spaces. 

## 8. Conclusion 

In this paper, we investigated strategic bargaining in concurrent multi-buyer markets, a setting where a single seller must navigate multiple private communication channels to identify and negotiate with the most profitable buyers. This environment presents a fundamental exploration-extraction trade-off, requiring an agent to balance its limited communication budget between market discovery and tactical bargaining. Our analysis revealed a critical misalignment in general-purpose Large Language Models: while these models are linguistically proficient, they act as “satisficers” in economic settings, settling for suboptimal agreements and failing to act as effective representatives for the firms they serve. We demonstrated that a specialized training recipe using Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) provides a solution to these behavioral shortcomings. By optimizing for objective economic outcomes—specifically profit extraction and strict adherence to reservation costs—the agent developed market-probing skills that generalize across different buyer distributions. Under this proposed framework, the 30B agent achieved a 4.7-fold improvement in mean reward over the closed-source gpt-5.1-high-reasoning baseline and a 7.5-fold improvement over the 1T-parameter 28 Liu et al.: LLM Negotiation with Verifiable Rewards 

open-source Kimi-K2.6-thinking model, while maintaining a perfect 0 .0% error rate regarding the reservation cost floor. Crucially, this performance stems from the emergence of proactive exploration strategies: the agent learns to utilize price anchoring and diagnostic probing to identify the buyer with the highest underlying budget, rather than merely responding to the first acceptable offer. The findings suggest that for specialized tasks in procurement, supply-chain management, and digital marketplaces, targeted reinforcement learning on verifiable environments is a more effective driver of utility than raw parameter scaling. As autonomous agents become increasingly prevalent in business-to-business networks and sequential-bargaining platforms, the framework presented here—linking objective terminal rewards to strategic outcomes—offers a scalable path toward creating reliable agents capable of maintaining goal consistency and maximizing value in complex, information-constrained markets. Appendix C: Prompt Templates 

To ensure the reproducibility of our results and to provide transparency regarding the agents’ oper-ating environments, this section details the comprehensive system instructions and environmental contexts utilized throughout our experiments. These templates define the behavioral constraints, structured action spaces, and informational partitions—such as the mandatory separation of private reasoning ( Thought ) from public dialogue ( Talk )—that facilitate the strategic challenges of the con-current negotiation task. C.1. Buyer System Prompts 

System Prompt for Buyer 

You are a buyer looking forward to buying things on your Shopping List from me, the seller. You have access to the seller’s Inventory List and you can bargain about the prices. Your task is to bargain with the seller and reach a deal with the price as low as possible in limited turns. You can only buy things on the Shopping List in the limited quantity. Use the codename of the product, instead of the title. You can only buy things that cost no more than your budget, otherwise, you should quit negotiating. Heterogeneity brings extra uncertainty. The same heterogeneity that confirms the distributional shift also injects uncertainty into the seller’s per-buyer inference in two concrete ways. (1) A higher first offer doesn’t necessarily indicate a higher budget. With the first-offer regulator disabled in the OOD setting, the opening price now mixes two signals: the buyer’s hidden budget and the buyer type’s intrinsic aggressiveness. Because iter-50’s mean opening sits at 0 .368 · Plist while iter-60’s sits at 0 .310 · Plist and iter-70’s at 0 .353 · Plist , the same observed opening price may be consistent with either a higher-budget buyer of a more aggressive type or a lower-budget buyer of a less aggressive type. (2) A lower-budget buyer is not necessarily an unprofitable target. Per-deal seller surplus is shaped not just by the buyer’s budget but by the buyer type’s willingness to concede headroom on the close: an iter-50 buyer extracts only 0 .636 of the available room ( Plist − C) on its deals, leaving 0.364 for the seller, while an iter-70 buyer extracts 0 .706, leaving only 0 .294 — a ∼1.2× gap in the seller’s share of the headroom captured per close. F.4. Strategic Analysis: Discovery vs. Signal-Following 

The transcripts in Section F.2 and Section F.3 illustrate a fundamental shift in how the agent perceives and interacts with a multi-buyer market. The divergence is characterized by three primary strategic behaviors: 

1. Overcoming First-Offer Bias. As shown in Table 13, the First-Offer Regulation ensures that all buyers open with nearly identical bids ( $9.00– $10.50), effectively masking their true valuations. 

• Untrained Agent: Exhibits a “greedy” lock-in behavior. It immediately targets Buyer 1 simply because that buyer’s opening of $10.50 was marginally higher than the others. By failing to engage Buyers 2 and 3, the agent remains ignorant of the fact that Buyer 3 actually possessed a much higher budget ( $40.11 vs $25.40). 

• Trained Agent: Treats the initial offers as non-informative. Instead of settling on a partner based on the Turn 1 signal, it treats the first three turns as an exploratory phase , sequentially anchoring at the list price ( $34.99) across all three channels to test for buyer elasticity. 

2. Diagnostic Probing and Budget Inference. The trained agent utilizes price anchoring 

not just to extract surplus, but as a diagnostic tool to resolve informational asymmetry. In the trained run, the seller observes the response of each buyer to the $34.99 anchor: 

• Buyer 1 and Buyer 3 (low and mid-budget) re-asserted their low bids or made minimal increases, signaling they were near their reservation limits. 

• Buyer 2 (the high-budget partner) responded to the anchor by jumping from $10.45 to $18.00. The agent correctly interprets this sudden escalation as a signal of high latent valuation, subsequently concentrating its remaining communication budget on that channel to maximize extracted surplus. 

3. Strategic Patience and Individual Rationality. A critical differentiator is the agent’s internalized adherence to the economic “floor.” 

• Untrained Agent: Demonstrates “satisficing” logic. It accepts a deal at $19.00 (just $0.25 above cost) on Turn 3. This reflects the “agreeableness” bias common in standard LLMs, where closing any deal is prioritized over optimizing for utility. 

• Trained Agent: Maintains strategic discipline. It repeatedly rejects offers below list price, even when they are profitable. By holding its anchor, it forces the high-budget buyer to escalate to

The trained agent views the concurrent setting as a unified discovery problem where information must be extracted before a deal can be executed.
