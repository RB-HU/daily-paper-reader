# CLQT: A Closed-Loop, Cost-Aware, Strategy-Consistent Benchmark for Diagnostic Evaluation of LLM Portfolio-Management Agents 

Bo Qu 1* · Mingguang Chen 2*

Abstract 

LLM agents are increasingly cast as autonomous portfolio managers, and bench-marks have moved their evaluation from financial question-answering to sequential trading [1, 2, 3]. Most closed-loop trading benchmarks still emphasize a leaderboard of returns over a fixed window, an incomplete proxy because a period’s return is dominated by the market path and apparent agent alpha can largely dissolve once look-ahead leakage is controlled, leaving mostly passive factor exposure [8, 9]. Such a ranking certifies neither the soundness of an agent’s reasoning, nor whether it holds a consistent strategy, nor the durability of its edge. We introduce CLQT ,which reframes closed-loop trading evaluation as diagnosis rather than ranking — following the broader turn in agent evaluation toward capability profiles over single scores [14, 23, 24] — an instrument that localizes where and why an agent’s process succeeds or fails. CLQT is a fully closed-loop , cost-aware , strategy-consistent , temporally-gated environment whose agents run a five-stage cycle — gather, synthesize, con-struct a mandate-aware portfolio, execute under realistic costs, then reflect — in the reason–act–reflect tradition [15, 16]. Each round emits a complete DecisionRound 

sealed into a recompute-verifiable hash chain , so every metric is reconstructable from the trail with no added instrumentation. Six pillars form the substrate: a hard 

TimeGate that treats leakage as a failed precondition, institutional transaction-and financing-cost modeling , strategy-consistency scoring across rounds, a 

three-tier consolidating memory , a Model-Context-Protocol tool layer [20], and a mandate-aware synthesis pipeline . The same agent runs as a constrained investment committee of specialized roles or a single full-autonomy orchestrator, making process scaffolding an experimental variable. From the audit trail we compute a five-axis capability scorecard (APM-CS) — Coherence, Acuity, Composure, Discipline, Reliability — each a finance instantiation of a general agent capability, with coherence judged partly by a held-out, out-of-cohort LLM to curb self-preference bias [13]. We validate it on a contamination-controlled multi-model backtest with an ablation grid and a live broker track on unseen, post-cutoff data, disciplining every claim with a repeated-run noise floor. CLQT thus separates 

outcome from capability across backtest and live; its lasting contribution is not a ranking of today’s models but a durable, extensible map of agent competencies and limitations. 

Keywords: large language models, agent benchmarks, closed-loop trading, strategy consistency, point-in-time evaluation, capability diagnostics.

## 1. Introduction 

Recent benchmarks for LLM-driven trading have advanced from static financial QA toward sequential decision-making [1, 2, 3]. Yet two gaps persist. The first is method-ological coverage: no existing benchmark simultaneously enforces point-in-time data semantics , models realistic transaction and financing costs across asset classes, eval-uates portfolio-level (not per-asset) decisions, measures strategy consistency across rounds, accumulates structured multi-tier memory , and treats tool orchestration 

as a benchmarkable capability. An agent that achieves high returns by inadvertently accessing future data, ignoring execution costs, or drifting from its mandate provides no credible signal about LLM capability. The second gap is interpretive, and it is the one this version foregrounds. The dom-inant evaluation idiom in LLM trading is the leaderboard : rank agents by Sharpe or cumulative return on a fixed period. But a leaderboard answers the wrong question for a research benchmark. A single quarter’s return is dominated by the realized mar-ket path and by which risk profile happened to suit it; a model can top the table for reasons unrelated to the quality of its reasoning. More fundamentally, a credible 

ranking of models would require testing dozens of models across many regimes and repeated runs — far beyond the scope of any single paper. We therefore argue that the durable contribution of a closed-loop benchmark is not a ranking but a diagnostic instrument: an apparatus that reads an agent’s full decision trail and localizes where in the gather →analyze →decide →execute →reflect loop, and why , the process breaks down. CLQT is built to be that instrument. Its design thesis is: a model should be called “superior” only when there is evidence of dominance across the relevant capability axes, and consistently across sub-periods — a bar that, by construction, is rarely met cleanly, which is exactly the point. The value the benchmark delivers is the map of limitations it produces, reusable as the model panel grows. To make the map concrete we introduce a 

five-axis diagnostic capability scorecard computed entirely from the audit trail 

(§6.4), and we render the trail trustworthy with a tamper-evident hash chain that any third party can recompute (§4.4). Figure 1 summarizes the design as a whole. The contributions of this paper are: 1. A diagnostic reframing of closed-loop trading evaluation: the contribution is an instrument that maps agent limitations, with an explicit standard (“consistent dominance across axes and sub-periods”) that resists premature ranking. 2. A five-axis capability scorecard (APM-CS) — Coherence, Acuity, Composure, Discipline, Reliability — each a finance instantiation of a general agent-evaluation capability, synthesized from existing audit fields with no new instrumentation. 3. A held-out LLM-as-judge for the Coherence axis (non-cohort, reasoning-enabled, cached), guarding against self-preference bias — with the headline coherence gap corroborated by a second, different-family held-out judge (Spearman ρ = 0.65), so the finding is not an artifact of one judge. 4. A tamper-evident audit hash chain : each round’s observation and action hashes are recomputed on verification and linked to the prior round’s action hash, so any post-hoc edit to a stored decision is detectable. 2Figure 1. CLQT graphical abstract — six design pillars wrap a five-stage closed loop around a three-tier memory core, sealed by a hash-linked DecisionRound audit trail. 

5. A closed-loop benchmark substrate with strict temporal integrity, institutional cost modeling, strategy-consistency measurement, three-tier memory, 19 MCP tools, mandate-aware synthesis, 3-D scouting, intertemporal sentiment trajectories, and verified live broker execution (carried forward and consolidated from prior versions). 6. A grounded demonstration : a chain-verified year-long bi-weekly campaign (5 models × 2 skill modes, repeated runs for the reliable models) plus a 13-configuration ablation grid (eight single-module / cost-tier variants and four multi-module cluster knockouts, repeated-run), a held-out Coherence judge, and a live broker track — showing the instrument separates outcome from capability, surfaces model-specific limitations, and explicitly bounds what the evidence can claim.

These benchmarks target different universes, periods, metrics, and goals, so a mark records the presence of a feature, not performance — and each prior benchmark is strong on the facet it was built for: leakage control (StockBench, DeepFund), live realism (LiveTradeBench, AI-Trader), multi-asset pipeline scoring (PortBench, LiveTradeBench). Several implement their target feature more thoroughly than CLQT does. CLQT’s claim is only breadth of coverage in one closed loop, which the matrix shows; it is not that CLQT is uniformly better than any of them. Recent work (2025–2026), read closely. Four strands of very recent work each sharpen one facet of the problem; reading what each does and does not measure is the clearest way to locate CLQT. 

Full-pipeline scoring. PortBench [7] is the closest in spirit — it evaluates the entire portfolio-management pipeline with correlation-aware scoring and a cross-stage error-propagation metric. But it scores that pipeline as a backtest over historical windows, stage-by-stage, rather than as a closed loop — without the persistent memory and tool use a live agent runs on. Its central move — decomposing where in the pipeline error originates rather than reporting a single score — is precisely the diagnostic stance CLQT adopts; CLQT extends it to a closed loop with memory, MCP tools, realistic costs and a live track (§3, §5), and replaces stage-accuracy with a five-axis capability profile. 

Returns-centric autonomous trading. StockBench [1] and AI-Trader [3] run autonomous stock-trading agents end-to-end and report returns-centric outcomes (cumulative return, Sharpe, drawdown) rather than a decision-process capability profile. Their demonstration that capable models can trade profitably motivates CLQT’s complementary question — 

is the profit coherent with the agent’s stated reasoning? — which §7 shows it frequently is not (the Sharpe leader is a 5-of-26-round artifact). 

Integrity and leakage. A focused line documents the failure mode CLQT’s TimeGate exists to prevent: Profit Mirage [8] shows that apparent LLM-agent alpha largely collapses 4once information leakage is removed, and memory-controlled masking [9] reports that returns under leakage-controlled evaluation are substantially explained by passive market and style exposure rather than stock-selection alpha. These results motivate CLQT’s hard temporal gate (§4) and per-run integrity ledger: we treat point-in-time enforcement as a precondition for a trustworthy number , not an optional safeguard. 

Memory as a measured variable. Memory-controlled trading benchmarks [9] (“from knowing to doing”) isolate the variable CLQT promotes to a first-class axis — whether an agent can convert recalled experience into improved action — which CLQT measures directly through its three-tier memory (§3.6) and the memory ablation (§7.7). 

Synthesis. No existing benchmark combines these facets. PortBench has the pipeline view but is backtest-only and not a closed loop; the returns benchmarks have autonomy and sometimes a live track but report a scalar; the integrity line fixes one precondition in isolation; the memory line isolates one variable. CLQT occupies the position none of them fills: a closed-loop, cost-aware, point-in-time, memory- and tool-bearing system that scores the decision process as a five-axis capability profile across both backtest and live (Table 1). 

2.2 Key Gaps Addressed No cost-awareness. Prior benchmarks either ignore transaction costs [1, 2, 3, 4] or apply a single flat commission [6]. CLQT implements four calibrated cost models (§3.4) across four asset classes and four cost tiers, plus daily financing-cost accrual. 

No cross-round learning evaluation. InvestorBench [5] mentions memory but does not formalize or measure it. CLQT’s three-tier memory (§3.6) stores structured event-action-outcome records that decay and consolidate. 

No tool synthesis evaluation. AI-Trader [3] mandates tool-only execution but does not evaluate orchestration quality. CLQT logs every MCP tool call with hashes and latency. 

No intertemporal information accumulation. All prior benchmarks treat news as single-round context. CLQT accumulates news across cycles in a persistent store (§3.8) and measures whether agents reason about sentiment dynamics .

No multi-dimensional candidate scouting. Prior work screens by price momentum alone, if at all. CLQT’s 3-D SCOUT stage (§3.3) adds earnings surprise and macro-correlation. 

No bridge-to-live execution. Most benchmarks stop at simulated fills. CLQT implements a complete live broker integration (§5.2) with paper-trading verification. 

2.3 The LLM-Agent Infrastructure CLQT Builds On 

Before it is a finance benchmark, CLQT is an LLM-agent system, and almost none of its machinery originates in financial AI — the loop, the memory, the tools, and the multi-agent committee are all adapted from the general agent-infrastructure literature of 2022–2025. We name that provenance explicitly, because it makes the design legible: 5each component exists to instantiate a mechanism that line of work showed to matter, and CLQT’s contribution is to make that mechanism measurable in a cost-bearing, temporally-gated decision process. 

Reasoning–acting loop. The five-stage loop follows the reasoning-and-acting paradigm of ReAct [15] and the self-critique loop of Reflexion [16]. CoALA (Cognitive Architectures for Language Agents) [19] supplies the unifying frame — language agents as production systems with explicit memory, action, and decision modules — that CLQT’s stage decomposition mirrors almost one-to-one (and connects directly to the ACT* grounding of §2.4). 

Memory — borrowed from general agent infrastructure, not finance. The intuition for CLQT’s three-tier memory comes from outside trading entirely. Generative Agents [17] showed that a memory stream with periodic reflection and relevance-weighted retrieval 

yields coherent long-horizon behavior in a sandbox of simulated humans; MemGPT [18] reframed context management as operating-system-style paging between a working context and external stores; and CoALA [19] organizes agent memory into working, episodic, semantic, and procedural tiers. CLQT adapts exactly this lineage — a memory stream that consolidates event →action →outcome records, decays, and is retrieved by relevance — to a portfolio ledger, with extracted “lessons” as reusable procedural memory. The novelty is not the mechanism but treating “does recalled experience improve the next decision?” as a measured axis rather than an assumed benefit (memory ablation, §7.7) — answering for trading the question memory-controlled benchmarks [9] pose in general. 

Tool use. That agents should act through tools rather than emit answers directly is the lesson of the tool-augmented-LLM line, exemplified by ToolLLM [21]; CLQT operational-izes it with a Model-Context-Protocol [20] tool layer (19 quantitative tools) — every tool call hashed, timed, and auditable, so tool use becomes evidence rather than a black box. 

Multi-agent committee and the structured scaffold. The role-decomposed committee (PM, Risk Officer, . . . ) draws on the general multi-agent-framework literature; the structured skill mode is inspired most directly by MetaGPT [22], whose central finding is that encoding standard operating procedures into the agent workflow reduces cascading error relative to free-form coordination. That result is the intuition behind CLQT’s structured-vs-autonomous contrast (§7.6): we do not assume scaffolding helps — we make the SOP-vs-autonomy trade-off an experimental variable and measure which models it helps, which it constrains, and on which axes. Within finance, the committee also builds on multi-agent trading frameworks like TradingAgents [10] and memory-augmented trading agents like FinMem [11]. 

Evaluation as a capability profile. The decision to report a profile rather than a scalar is itself borrowed: HELM [14] argued for holistic, multi-metric evaluation over a single leaderboard number, and agent benchmarks such as AgentBench [23] and τ -bench [24] score agents across tasks and trajectories rather than final answers. CLQT is the finance instantiation of that turn — five axes, a hash-chained audit trail, and a held-out judge — specialized to a closed-loop, cost-bearing portfolio process. Evaluation methodology and judge design. Beyond the trading-benchmark literature, CLQT borrows from the broader practice of capability diagnostics in LLM/agent evalu-6ation: reporting a profile of skills rather than a scalar, controlling for confounds with held-out judges and ablations, and being explicit about statistical power (repeated runs, confidence intervals, sensitivity of conclusions to setup). Recent work on LLM-as-judge [13] cautions about self-preference bias and the need for non-cohort judges and rubric grounding. CLQT instantiates these practices in finance: the five-axis scorecard is a capability profile; the Coherence judge is held out from the evaluated cohort; ablations and a repeated-run protocol bound module-importance claims; and reliability (format adherence, completion, truncation) is a first-class axis rather than an auxiliary note. This aligns with procedure-aware agent evaluation [12], which scores the correctness of the process rather than only task success. CLQT’s held-out reasoning judge — cached, non-cohort, and reported alongside the mechanical signal–action agreement it system-atically diverges from (the +0.33/+0.34 gap of §5.5) — is a finance instantiation of that stance: the divergence between the two, essentially the same on backtest and live, is itself the diagnostic. Architecturally, CLQT’s role-decomposed committee and reflect-and-learn stage build on multi-agent financial frameworks like TradingAgents [10] and memory-augmented trading agents like FinMem [11], and follow the ReAct [15] / Reflexion [16] reasoning-acting-reflection paradigm; its three-tier memory connects to general agentic-memory work [18, 19] and its tool layer to the MCP standard [20]. CLQT is complementary to broad financial-LLM benchmark suites in treating the decision process itself as a measurable capability profile. 

2.4 Theoretical and Cognitive Foundations 

CLQT’s architecture is not ad hoc: each pillar instantiates an established model of decision-making or cognition, and several of the paper’s findings are most naturally read through these lenses. We make the mappings explicit because they are what lets a trading 

benchmark speak to general agent behavior. 

The closed loop ←− decision-cycle theory. The gather →analyze →decide →execute →reflect loop is a finance instantiation of Boyd’s OODA cycle (Observe–Orient–Decide–Act) [26] and of the classic three-level situation-awareness model — perception of task-relevant elements, comprehension of their meaning, and projection of their near-future implica-tions. The mapping is direct: Intelligence Acquisition is perception, Signal Synthesis is comprehension, Portfolio Construction is projection-and-decision. This grounding also 

defines the Acuity axis (§6.4): a Level-1 (perception) failure — attending to salient-but-irrelevant cues — is exactly what Acuity measures when it contrasts an agent’s response to informative signals against a volatility distractor .

Reasoning vs. acting ←− Anderson’s declarative/procedural distinction. Anderson’s ACT* theory of cognition [25] separates declarative knowledge (factual propositions in semantic memory) from procedural knowledge (condition–action production rules compiled only through repeated practice). Anderson’s central claim — that procedural skill is acquired by compiling declarative precursors through application , not by declarative retrieval alone — predicts a specific deficit in text-trained models: they encode the propositions of a strategy (declarative) but not the production rules that let those propositions govern an action (procedural), because the compilation step requires active practice a text corpus cannot supply. This is the theoretical basis for CLQT’s central Coherence axis and, we 7argue in §8, the most parsimonious explanation of its headline finding — the systematic gap between what an agent states and what it does .

Reflect-and-learn ←− experiential-learning theory. The Attribution & Lesson Extraction stage and three-tier memory operationalize an experiential-learning cycle (concrete expe-rience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation) and follow the ReAct [15] / Reflexion [16] reasoning–acting–reflection paradigm; the memory tiers themselves draw on the classic episodic/semantic distinction and recent agentic-memory work [18, 19]. Reflection is, in Anderson’s terms, the benchmark’s attempt to supply the missing practice — and whether it helps is an empirical question the memory ablation (§7.7) is built to answer. 

Composure ←− behavioral finance. The Composure axis measures overreaction to volatil-ity — the LLM-agent analogue of the classic overreaction hypothesis from behavioral finance and of noise-driven trading: a disciplined agent should not churn its book in response to transient volatility. Reading agent churn as overreaction connects CLQT’s per-round turnover-vs-volatility regression to a long empirical tradition. 

Synthesis. Grounding the five axes in cognition and decision theory is what makes them portable — the finance task is the instrument , but the capabilities it measures are not finance-specific, which is why a portfolio-management benchmark can function as a general agent diagnostic. We map each axis to its construct in §8.2. 

## 3. System Design 

3.1 Architecture Overview 

CLQT operates as a five-stage closed loop executed by TradingEngine , which orchestrates Protocol modules through a six-agent architecture . The loop is the OODA / situation-awareness decision cycle of §2.4 made executable — perception (IA), comprehension (MSS), projection-and-decision (MAPC), action (COE), and reflection (ALE) — and the role decomposition follows the multi-agent financial-trading line [10] while the reflect-and-learn stage instantiates the ReAct [15] / Reflexion [16] paradigm. Each stage is a Python Protocol interface — implementations can be swapped without inheritance. All quantitative analysis is performed through 19 typed tools defined to the Model-Context-Protocol (MCP) standard and grouped into four namespaces (market-data, quant-tools, news-sentiment, execution); within the benchmark these run 

in-process against the underlying quant and data libraries — we adopt the MCP tool-definition interface and schemas, not a networked server transport — so every analytical step is an auditable, schema-checked tool call rather than free-form text generation (full inventory in Appendix B, Table B1). The five stages are: 1. Intelligence Acquisition (IA) — gather market, news, macro, and asset-class-specific data; scout universe candidates. 2. Multi-Source Signal Synthesis (MSS) — produce signals, risk assessments, and factor decompositions. 3. Mandate-Aware Portfolio Construction (MAPC) — propose and validate target allocations against the investment mandate. 84. Cost-Optimal Order Execution (COE) — generate and execute orders with cost-minimizing strategies. 5. Attribution & Lesson Extraction (ALE) — attribute performance, extract structured lessons, update memory. The atomic unit of evaluation is the DecisionRound :

3.2 Six-Agent Architecture 

CLQT employs a six-agent architecture where specialized agents receive agent skills 

— structured workflow protocols — that define the sequence of MCP tool calls and reasoning steps. Each agent (Research, Analyst, PM, Risk Officer, Execution, Reflection) maps onto a stage of the loop and is granted only the tool subset its role needs; this 

stage-to-agent-to-tool mapping is shown in Figure 2, and the exact skill files and tool assignments are available from the authors on request. 

Structured vs. Autonomous Skill Modes CLQT supports a skill rigidity ablation 

via agent.skill-mode :

• structured : Six specialized agents with restricted tool subsets matching their stage role and prescribed skill protocols. Agents reason within the workflow — formulating views, adjusting signal weights — but cannot change the workflow topology. A Risk Officer gate may reject and request a revision. 

• autonomous : A single AutonomousOrchestratorAgent owns the entire pipeline from SCOUT through REFLECT, receiving all tools and only a mandate document, constraint set, and required output schema. It decides which tools to call, in what order, how often, and whether to skip stages. Both modes use real agentic tool calling : LLM tool-use blocks are executed in-process against the actual quant and data libraries, with results fed back in a multi-turn loop. This is not simulated tool use or pre-assembled prompt data. (As §6.4/§7 detail, this mode switch is a confound for two axes — Discipline and Acuity — that we control by comparing within mode.)

## 4. Data Integrity 

4.1 TimeGate: Point-in-Time Enforcement 

All data access passes through TimeGate , enforcing ∀d ∈ D t : timestamp (d) ≤ tas_of .For live mode an intraday_cutoff freezes access N minutes before the decision timestamp. The TimeGate applies uniformly to all modalities, including memory retrieval and the 

NewsStore supplement pass. 14 4.2 Contamination Prevention 

Post-cutoff windows. Per-model eligibility filtering ensures backtest data postdates each model’s training cutoff. Universe integrity. Point-in-time universe membership avoids survivorship bias. 

4.3 Contamination Detection 

Two complementary probes are implemented: canary documents (synthetic disclosures with unique identifiers detected via substring + fuzzy match) and future-shift leakage probes (paired baseline/probe decisions with normalized L1 weight divergence). These detection probes are part of the substrate but were not exercised in this single, benign campaign; contamination resistance here rests on the point-in-time gate and per-model cutoff filtering (§4.2), the live post-cutoff track (§5.5), and the verified hash chain (§4.4). Active adversarial/canary probing to stress the capability axes directly is a hardening direction (§8.8). 

4.4 Tamper-Evident Audit Trail 

Each DecisionRound is sealed into a recompute-verifiable hash chain : a content hash over the inputs the agent saw and a content hash over the decision it took, with each round’s hash folded into the next so the run forms an append-only ledger. Verification re-derives every hash from the persisted record and checks both that it matches the stored value and that the chain linkage holds; any post-hoc edit — a changed weight, a deleted order, a reordered round — breaks the chain at the first altered record. The audit trail is therefore tamper-evident : a reviewer handed only the persisted per-run store can independently confirm that the recorded decisions are exactly those the engine produced. Every campaign run passes chain verification end-to-end. 

## 5. Backtest vs. Live Evaluation 

5.1 Backtest Track: Contamination-Aware Period Design 

Rather than a single fixed period, CLQT assigns models to evaluation groups by training cutoff: Group B (cutoff > 2 years ago) → 2-year backtest from the cutoff; Group A (cutoff > 1 year ago) → 1-year backtest. Backtest parameters: 252-day warmup; configurable initial capital (default $1,000,000); bi-weekly rebalance (the 26-round campaign cadence, §7.2); LOW cost tier by default; deterministic given the configuration. In this paper’s campaign every evaluated model’s cutoff precedes the single 1-year window (§7.2), so the longer 2-year Group B path, though supported, is not exercised by this cohort. Universe and starting book. The investable universe is derived from the S&P 100 — large, liquid, survivorship-controlled names with point-in-time membership (a symbol enters only once it was actually in the index as of the decision date). Each run opens from a core book of roughly 14 equal-weighted holdings carried as the starting portfolio, against a candidate pool of ~58 additional names ( ≈72 symbols in total) that the SCOUT stage (§3.3) ranks for rotation. The universe is deliberately broad enough to permit meaningful 15 selection and turnover, yet bounded so the cohort stays comparable across models and the news/fundamentals channels remain tractable within free-tier API limits. 

5.2 Live Track: Broker Paper-Trading Architecture 

A live track serves two purposes: (1) genuine contamination resistance — decisions execute on data that postdates every model’s training cutoff — and (2) a check that the benchmark’s execution abstractions map onto a real broker’s order lifecycle. CLQT integrates a commercial broker’s paper-trading API. Each session gathers and decides with the decision timestamp fixed to the Sunday before the Monday open, covering weekend news and matching the Monday backtest round’s as-of time so the two tracks are comparable on identical data; it then previews and submits orders at the open, polls fills, reconciles the resulting book from the broker’s positions and cash ledger, and locks the portfolio between rebalances. A dedicated execution agent optimizes only cost and fill quality — the target weights are fixed by the upstream decision. The live track thus executes real fills on genuinely unseen, post-cutoff data; the broker integration is documented and available from the authors on request. 

5.3 Rebalancing vs. Re-Optimization 

CLQT distinguishes rebalancing (mechanical weight restoration) from re-optimization (full pipeline producing a new target) via three rebalance modes — calendar, threshold, and agent-discretion — enabling evaluation of when -to-trade judgment. 

5.4 Cross-Track Comparison Protocol 

The two tracks are run on the same dates and configuration and reported on the same metric suite, so discrepancies are attributable: a backtest Sharpe exceeding live by more than ~0.2 flags possible contamination, while fill-price divergence between simulated and live execution gauges cost-model calibration. 

5.5 Live Track: Decision-Level Validation (Two-Week Campaign) 

The live track ran 2026-06-15 → 06-26 (nine valid NYSE days across two consecutive weeks) as a daily-rebalance campaign on a shared broker paper account: 6 newer “thinking” models (minimax-m3, deepseek-v4-pro, qwen3.7-plus, gpt-5.4-mini, gemini-3.5-flash, claude-sonnet-4.6) × {structured, autonomous} = 12 concurrent virtual-ledger sessions, each a $1M slice at medium reasoning. (2026-06-19, Juneteenth, is a NYSE holiday; that round priced on prior-session data and is dropped via a published exclusion manifest — an integrity detail enforced by an exchange-calendar gate.) Each ledger is pre-loaded with an equal-weight core book at the prior close, so only the daily delta reaches the broker; an end-of-day two-layer flatten returns the shared account to flat. What the gap measures, and why coherence scores low. The two halves of Coherence ask different-difficulty questions. Agreement checks only the direction of each proposed trade against the sign of that name’s informative composite — a low bar a coin-flip clears at 0.5 and modest skill lifts to ~0.6. The judge asks whether the specific weights are a coherent consequence of the agent’s own stated analysis — and the cohort fails it: across the campaign the held-out judge’s mean score is 2.12 of 5 (mode 2; normalized 0.28). The gap is therefore not a quirk of one metric but the distance between two competencies — aligning with external signals versus keeping one’s allocation consistent with one’s own narrative . Reading the cached judge rationales, the low scores are concrete and checkable, recurring in five patterns: (i) direct contradiction (“analysis says TSLA should be reduced 

but its weight change is +1.7%”); (ii) equal-weight collapse that ignores the differentiation the analysis emphasized (“near-equal-weight 7.1–7.3% across all names . . . CAT +3.74 vs LIN −3.59 receive essentially identical weights, incoherent with a signal-driven mean-variance analysis”); (iii) name mismatch (the thesis names AVGO/GS/MS/NEM; the book holds PG/AMT/NEE/AMZN); (iv) weights anti-correlated with the cited signals (overweighting the most bearish name at 12% while the strongest bullish name gets 0.2%); and (v) no thesis at all (an autonomous →MAPC placeholder with “no thesis for the specific name selection”). This is the stating-versus-doing deficit made concrete. 

Where the incoherence enters. Part of it is the agent’s narrative not matching even its own intent; part is the machinery . The judge scores the post-construction target, which the quant optimizer and the constrained projection have already reshaped — so the equal-weight collapse (the optimizer flattening a tactical tilt) and the cap-hits (the projection clipping) are partly system effects, not pure reasoning failures. The instrument records proposed (pre-MAPC intent) separately from projected (post-machinery) precisely 18 so the two can be attributed (§3.7); the extreme tell is the bare-workflow agent (§7.7) whose entire rationale is “Optimizer: risk_parity . . . Accepted 0 adjustments” — there is no thesis for the allocation to cohere with. Read at the level of the agent-in-the-system, then, the gap measures a real reasoning–action consistency deficit: a competent reader, handed the agent’s analysis, would not derive the book it produced. 

What the cross-track agreement does and does not establish. That the gap reproduces on genuinely post-cutoff live data rules out memorization — a leakage artifact would shrink off-distribution, and it does not. More telling than the headline match is what moves versus what sticks : the component levels rise with model generation (newer live models judge higher, e.g. deepseek 0.25 → 0.44, and agreement rises too) while the difference stays pinned — agents narrate better and point better in proportion, leaving the gap generation-invariant, the signature ACT* (§2.4) predicts. We are deliberate about the limits, though: even over nine live rounds the two gaps are close, not identical 

(0.326 vs 0.339) — strong enough that the small-sample “fortunate rounding” caveat of the week-1 pilot no longer applies, but still consistent with the same value rather than a proof of equality; and because both tracks share the metric, the match is instrument-consistency, a statement about the apparatus measuring stably. The strongest robustness check is therefore the second judge (below), which rules out a different failure mode — one judge’s idiosyncrasy — than the cross-track test does. Read together they make the deficit reproducible and judge-robust , with the intrinsic-limitation interpretation the most parsimonious reading rather than a settled result. 

What the cross-track does not claim. The live and backtest cohorts are different model versions , so this is instrument-consistency, not same-model generalization; family-level traits reflect model and condition (e.g., gemini’s newer version intermittently holds 

live, §7.11, where the older one was active in backtest). Two weeks of live data also revise two week-1 reads that four rounds could not settle: gemini-3.5-flash, the week-1 Composure floor, lifts to the cohort’s highest Composure once its week-2 low-turnover stretch enters the volatility-overreaction regression — the floor is in fact minimax-m3 (and gpt-5.4-mini); and gpt-5.4-mini’s scaffold edge reverses to favor autonomy. With nine rounds the Composure and Acuity axes resolve well enough to rank, though they remain the shortest-horizon axes and are read with more caution than Coherence, Reliability, and Discipline. 

Robustness to the judge. 20 6.4 The Five-Axis Diagnostic Capability Scorecard (APM-CS) 

The scorecard is the instrument’s read-out. It scores five capability axes from the audit trail, ordered high →low by how fundamental they are to competent autonomous portfolio management. Each axis is a finance instantiation of a general agent-evaluation capability, so a finding about a trading agent generalizes to a statement about the model’s reasoning. Outcome (returns) is not an axis — it is a result explained by the axes. 

① Coherence — general capability: global, systems-level reasoning vs. local greedy pattern-matching; an analogue of systematic alignment vs. turn-by-turn instruction-following at the level of reasoning (does the conclusion follow from the agent’s own analysis?). Finance: the chosen allocation follows from the agent’s stated research and the signals it cited. Synthesis: 

D1 = 0 .5 Asig + 0 .5 Jjudge 

where Asig is signal-action agreement: the fraction of the agent’s proposed trades (mapc_delta_proposed , pre-MAPC) whose direction matches the sign of that name’s cross-sectional informative composite (below). Jjudge is the held-out LLM judge’s score (§6.4.1), normalized to [0,1]. Reveals shallow or self-contradictory reasoning that returns hide. 

② Acuity (information attention / acuteness) — general: salience attention vs. distrac-tion, i.e. endurance under polluted / misleading context. Finance: attend to informative signals amid a large noisy universe and a flood of news. Synthesis , on proposed adjust-ments (not final weights, which are confounded by the optimizer/MAPC): 

D2 = ρs(∆ prop , I ) − ρs(∆ prop , N )

where ρs is Spearman correlation, I = z(m12 −1) + z(esurp ) + z(nsent ) is the informative composite, and N = z(σrealized ) is the volatility-noise distractor. Each term is z-scored cross-sectionally per round and averaged over rounds with ≥3 proposed trades. Positive 

⇒ the agent’s intent tracks alpha-bearing signals more than the high-variance, low-alpha distractor; near-zero or negative ⇒ chasing noise. 

③ Composure — general: robustness / consistency under input perturbation vs. reactive overcorrection. Finance: respond to volatility and misleading data with discipline, not churn. Synthesis (the mean of its legs, not a product): 

D3 = mean (1 − sreact , c cons )

where sreact is the (squashed, non-negative) regression coefficient of per-round one-way turnover on a market-volatility proxy (cross-sectional mean realized_vol ), and ccons is the strategy-consistency leg when available; a high slope means turnover rises sharply with volatility. We score Composure as the average of its legs rather than their product for two reasons made concrete by the campaign: a product lets one weak leg collapse the whole axis (it sent a single model’s Composure to ~0), and — because the strategy-consistency 

leg is produced by the memory module — a product made Composure uncomputable under the memory ablation (§7.7). The mean is robust to a single low leg and falls back 21 to the memory-free calm leg when consistency is absent, so Composure is defined for 

every configuration including memory-off ablations. (Style-drift is ≈0 cohort-wide, so it is reported separately rather than carried as a non-discriminating leg.) Reveals models that overreact to volatile or misleading inputs. 

④ Discipline — general: systematic alignment to a standing mandate vs. turn-by-turn instruction-following — does the agent self-regulate to a policy even absent a hard guardrail? Finance: respect caps/turnover/risk budgets without relying on the MAPC clip. Synthesis (bounded to [0 , 1] ): 

D4 = 12 rself + 12 (1 − dclip )

where rself is the self-constrained rate: the fraction of rounds whose target satisfies constraints pre-MAPC. dclip = min (1 , ¯aclip /κ ) is clip dependence, measuring reliance on the reconciler to force the book inside the mandate ( κ = 10 , a fully-saturated structured round; ¯aclip is the mean number of clipped MAPC adjustments). Both legs lie in 

[0 , 1] , so Discipline does too. (A turnover-cap leg used in earlier drafts was dropped: campaign turnover ~0.15 sits far below any real cap, so the leg was a non-discriminating constant.) Mode confound: RO-approval is meaningful only in structured ; autonomous 

sets risk_approved=True by construction — so Discipline is compared within mode. 

⑤ Reliability — general: protocol/format adherence + operational robustness + ef-ficiency — the analogue of an agent completing a tool-use task in valid form, every time, at reasonable cost. Finance: close the loop in valid schema at acceptable compute. 

Synthesis: 

D5 = rcomplete (1 − rparse ) (1 − rtrunc )

where rcomplete is completion rate, rparse is parse-fail rate, and rtrunc is truncation rate. Efficiency is reported beside it ($/round, reasoning-tokens/round, latency).

sub-period × run) with rank-stability (Kendall’s τ ) are a planned broader-run output (§8.8). The thesis bar — superior = consistent dominance across axes and sub-periods

6.4.1 Held-out LLM-as-Judge for Coherence The judge component of Coherence is scored by a held-out, non-cohort model — i.e. a model not in the evaluated set — to avoid the self-preference bias documented for LLM-as-judge. For each round the judge receives a compact, audit-derived prompt: the agent’s stated analysis (research brief and/or optimizer rationale), the top names by informative-composite strength with their underlying signals, the chosen target weights, and the proposed weight changes. It 22 returns a 1–5 score for the single question “does the chosen allocation follow coherently from the stated analysis and signals?” — explicitly told to ignore profitability and to penalize self-contradiction and untethered allocations. Scores are cached by round_id (so re-runs never re-bill) and reasoning is enabled on the judge model deliberately: coherence adjudication is nuanced and the held-out judge is a reasoning model, so we trade a few cents per round for adjudication quality. Bumping the prompt version invalidates the cache so all rounds are re-judged consistently when the rubric changes. Two-judge protocol (primary + corroboration). The primary judge is minimax-m3 — it produces every Coherence number reported in §7 and §5. Because the agreement ↔judge coherence gap (§5.5) is the instrument’s strongest claim, we corroborate it with a second ,different-family held-out judge, GLM-5.2 (also non-cohort and reasoning-enabled), scored into a separate cache over the same rounds. The two judges agree closely at the round level (Spearman ρ = 0.65, 97 % within one point on the 1–5 rubric) and the gap reproduces under the swap (§8.7), so the headline finding is a property of the allocations, not of one judge. Both judges are non-cohort; the second is used only as a robustness check, not blended into the primary score. 

6.5 Behavioral Interpretability: Method 

The same audit trail that powers the quantitative scorecard (§6.4) also supports a quali-tative, behavioral read — extracting each model’s decision behaviors, stated reasoning, and recurring patterns into a human-legible picture of its “decision personality.” This is the tradition of work that probes LLM black boxes through their observable actions and self-reports rather than their weights, and CLQT is unusually well-suited to it because every round is a complete, hash-chained, verifiable record (§4.4). Captured per round, per model × mode, with no new instrumentation (the exact audit fields behind each are mapped in Appendix D, Table D1): 

• Stated reasoning — the agent’s own narrative account of why an allocation was chosen: research and analyst briefs, the allocation rationale, risk-officer notes, and the post-round reflection. 

• Metacognition and learning — how a model abstracts experience into typed, reusable “lessons”, and whether those lessons are later retrieved back into context. 

• Intent vs. guardrails — the gap between what the model proposed and what the reconciler allowed (in a sampled structured run the agent proposed 12–14 weight moves per round and 7–12 were clipped — a quantifiable “intent meets institutional guardrails” signal). 

• Inter-agent dynamics — how the PM and Risk Officer negotiate, and when the committee overrides itself. 

• Information foraging — each model’s characteristic data-gathering strategy: which tools it calls, in what order, and how widely it explores the candidate universe. 

• Cognitive effort — where a model “thinks hardest” (reasoning-token and latency budget per role) and whether that effort tracks decision quality. 

• Struggle signatures — where and why a model fails to produce a valid decision: retry loops, parse failures, and constraint violations. 23 Method. Thematic coding of the reasoning corpus (optionally LLM-assisted via a held-out 

summarizer mirroring the judge protocol in §6.4.1), frequency analysis of intent-vs-clip and committee overrides, per-model tool-use fingerprints, lesson formation and reuse, and illustrative anecdotes — each tied to a hash-verified round so it is reproducible. Scope (honest caveat). We analyze stated and observable reasoning artifacts and be-havioral metadata, not raw hidden chain-of-thought: providers generally do not return the thinking trace, and we persist reasoning-token counts , not the trace text. The interpretability is therefore of behavior + self-report — auditable and reproducible (every claim pinned to a hash-chained round), but situated one layer above the model’s hidden cognition. We treat this scoping as a feature, not a gap: it keeps the “human reading of the AI” grounded in verifiable evidence rather than unfalsifiable introspection. 

## 7. Experiments 

The experiments here are the full 1-year campaign — still not a model ranking, but a 

map of limitations across a 5-model cohort. The purpose is to show that the five-axis scorecard discriminates decision quality where outcome metrics conflate it, surfaces model-specific failures invisible to returns, and — by the instrument’s own standard (consistent dominance across axes and sub-periods ) — resists premature ranking even with repeated-run evidence. Rows report means over repeated runs; bootstrapped CIs and Kendall-τ rank-stability are a planned broader-run pass (§8.8). The method, the axes, and the audit machinery are the contribution. 

7.1 Research Questions 

• RQ1 (instrument validity): Does the scorecard discriminate decision quality where outcome metrics conflate it? 

• RQ2 (capability ̸ = outcome): Is the Sharpe leader the capability leader, and does any single configuration dominate all five axes? 

• RQ3 (model-specific limitations): Does the audit trail surface model-specific failures invisible to returns? 

• RQ4 (scaffold effect): How does the structured ↔autonomous skill mode shape the capability profile? 

• RQ5 (module importance): Do single-run module ablations yield clean importance claims, or is the repeated-run campaign required? 

• RQ6 (cost realism): Are the cost tiers calibrated, and how sensitive are conclusions to them? 

• RQ7 (passive comparison): Net of costs, does any agent beat the cap-weighted index and the broader passive suite?

(ix) The regime does much of the work — which masks both module value and model risk-taking. Every number above is measured in one benign, rising market: over the campaign SPY returned +27% with only a −8.9% drawdown and a calm VIX (median 17; above 25 on just 7% of days). Three consequences follow, and each is a reason the ablation ∆ are small. First, the market — not the modules — drives the returns: in a trending bull almost any reasonable long-only book posts a strong Sharpe and clears the defensive baselines, so removing a module cannot move returns much — there is little danger for the analytical or learning machinery to steer the agent away from. Second, the modules whose value is most regime-contingent are exactly the ones this period mutes: the information stack ( news-sentiment / world-macro / asset-class-data ) earns its keep at turning points — spotting deteriorating breadth or a regime shift — not while riding a trend; the learning loop ( memory / reflection ) pays off by adapting across 

regimes, and a single stable trend gives it little to adapt to; and the covariance-aware optimizer ( advanced-quant ) matters most when correlations spike in a sell-off, not when everything rises together. Their flat ablation ∆ here is therefore weak evidence of irrelevance and strong evidence that this regime cannot test them. Third — and most consequential — a rising market masks risk-taking shortcomings the scorecard catches but returns do not. In a bull run the configurations that look best on raw return are those that take the most risk (full exposure, momentum-chasing, concentration), while the prudent de-risking the agents actually exhibit (half the index’s drawdown, Table 9) 

costs return and looks worse — so a returns leaderboard in this regime rewards exactly the recklessness a drawdown would punish and penalizes the risk management that would protect it. qwen3 ·structured’s 5-round “Sharpe 9.82” (§7.3) is the extreme: an artifact a bull-market returns ranking flatters and the Reliability / risk axes expose. The benign 35 window is thus not merely a power limit on the ablations (stress bucket ≈ 4–5 of 26 rounds, §8.7) but a structural reason the capability and risk-adjusted axes are necessary — they are what stays diagnostic when the regime makes returns uninformative, or worse, misleading. 

7.8 Methodology note: reasoning needs output headroom 

A test-time-compute model can look like an incapable portfolio manager when it is merely 

truncated — a tight output cap severs its chain-of-thought mid-stream. We therefore standardize on a generous output budget and report cap-hit, truncation, rate-limit, and parse-fail as first-class reliability telemetry. Without that telemetry a benchmark misattributes a budget or infrastructure artifact (qwen3’s incompletion, §7.5) to a capability gap — exactly the misread a returns-only or single-configuration view invites. 

7.9 RQ7: Net of Costs, Agents Clear the Defensive Baselines but Not the Index Cleanly 

Over the full bi-weekly year, the reliable structured configurations clear a median ~6 of 8 passive baselines (deepseek 6.3, haiku 6.0, gemini 5.7; Figure 12) — a materially stronger showing than the single-quarter design pilot, where no agent beat SPY. But the beaten set is dominated by the suite’s defensive / diversified members (IEF bonds, risk-parity, 60/40, equal-weight); the cap-weighted equity index remains the hardest bar, and the autonomous configurations clear far fewer (gemini and haiku autonomous: just 1/8). The honest reading the instrument enforces — scoring every config against the 

whole suite, not a hand-picked benchmark — is that under realistic costs these agents reliably beat the defensive baselines but do not uniformly clear the cap-weighted index, and autonomy (autonomous mode) markedly weakens even that. (qwen3 ·structured’s 8/8 is excluded as a 5-round artifact, §7.3.) The index gap is a de-risking trade, not a deficit. “Does not beat the cap-weighted index” understates what the risk-adjusted read shows in Table 9: against SPY the agents run lower volatility and roughly half the maximum drawdown ( −4.1% vs −8.9%) at the same Sharpe and ≈2× the Calmar. The shortfall in absolute return is the price of deliberate diversification away from the index’s mega-cap concentration — a trade that loses a little in a concentrated bull run and wins decisively in a drawdown. So the honest statement is not “no skill vs the index” but “a risk-managed profile the index does not have”: the agents convert a passive long-only mandate into a lower-drawdown, comparable-Sharpe strategy, and — per the ablation diagnostics — this risk discipline is intrinsic to the constrained construction and survives even the bare-workflow stripping. The Composure axis (§7.6) is the in-instrument measure of exactly this behavior; a returns leaderboard, fixated on the −2.4% vs SPY, would miss it.

7.11 Behavioral Interpretability: Findings 

Because every round stores the agent’s stated analysis, rationale, reflection, and parse status, the trail lets us read behavior , not just scores — and the most instructive live behavior is an autonomous-mode convergence to hold (no rebalance) that, examined 37 closely, decomposes into two distinct, model-specific mechanisms. The structured scaffold prevents both. 

Two roads to the same hold (Fig. 13). Across the nine live days, exactly two of six models ever produce a null-strategy hold, and only in autonomous mode: gemini-3.5-flash holds 6 of 9 rounds and minimax-m3 5 of 9, while the other four autonomous models — and all six in structured mode — hold 0%. The two roads are distinct: - gemini-3.5-flash — tool-turn-budget exhaustion. Its reflection carries a complete mean-variance thesis (ten high-momentum names, a risk-on regime read), yet the committed round records only a truncation marker: the autonomous agent spends its tool-turn budget exploring and never serializes a final allocation, so an empty-weights fallback holds the book. It reasoned but did not deliver in budget . - minimax-m3 — governance-schema-adherence failure. The round is rejected for emitting schema-invalid output — an empty value where the output schema required a list — and with no valid decision it falls back to hold. (Held rounds even carry longer rationales: the model produces more text, but malformed.) Intermittent, independent, recoverable — not a one-way collapse. The first live week reads like a clean “day-1 trade, then lock-up”: both models traded day 1 and then held days 2–4 in lockstep, which a short pilot would report as a deterministic day-2 collapse. The second week overturns that reading. The holds are intermittent (both models trade on several later days — gemini on 06-23/06-24, minimax on 06-23/06-25/06-26 — so the failure is recoverable , not absorbing), and they are independent: in week 2 the two models hold on different days (06-24 minimax holds while gemini trades; 06-25/06-26 gemini holds while minimax trades), with no synchrony. The diagnostically correct summary is therefore a per-model autonomous hold-rate (gemini 0.67, minimax 0.56) driven by a stochastic, model-specific fragility the autonomous scaffold exposes — not a shared, monotone “models stop trading after day 1.” This is exactly the kind of claim a longer live horizon is needed to get right, and a caution against over-reading any few-round live result, our own week-1 pilot included. Scaffold magnifies vs. constrains (the structured–autonomous thread). The same minimax-m3 in structured mode still logs parse failures yet keeps trading: the staged pipeline (Risk-Officer review, MAPC reconciliation) contains a malformed sub-output and recovers, where the single-shot autonomous path lets the same failure collapse the whole round. This is a concrete instance of a recurring theme — structure converts a model’s raw fragility into graceful degradation, at the cost of the autonomy that, for the more capable models, yields the cohort’s best decisions (claude-sonnet-4.6 and qwen3.7-plus both score markedly higher autonomous than structured — 69.1 vs 52.7 and 61.8 vs 41.8 — and deepseek-v4-pro scores equally in both). Whether an agent is best deployed as a disciplined expert-human proxy (structured) or given autonomy to exceed human heuristics is therefore model-dependent, not settled — a question we return to in §8. ## 8. Discussion 

8.1 The Instrument, Not the Leaderboard 

The campaign makes the central argument concretely: the capability leader (deepseek ·structured, APM-CS 72.4) and the nominal Sharpe “winner” (qwen3 ·structured, 9.82) are different configurations — and the latter is a 5-of-26-round artifact (Reliability 0.19) the scorecard exposes rather than crowns. A leaderboard would report a spurious number and stop; the scorecard instead returns a profile for each — qwen3 attends to the 39 noise distractor over the alpha signal (negative Acuity) and cannot sustain the structured pipeline (null PM responses from truncation + provider rate-limiting — operational, not cognitive, §7.5); haiku ·structured posts the best reliable Sharpe (3.20) yet the lowest Coherence (0.15); deepseek leads on balance but is only mid-pack on Sharpe; and the same model’s profile shifts sharply with scaffold (deepseek 72.4 structured → 36.7 autonomous). These are research-actionable statements (where to intervene), not a rank. They also explain why we decline to rank: no configuration dominates all five axes, and a credible cross-regime ranking would need an order of magnitude more models, regimes, and repeated runs — out of scope by design, and exactly the larger evidence base the instrument is meant to support over time. 

8.2 Capability Axes as General Agent Diagnostics 

Each axis is deliberately a finance instantiation of a general capability, which lets trading findings speak to model behavior at large. Coherence is systems-level reasoning vs. greedy local moves — the gap between an agent’s stated analysis and its action; the judge/agreement divergence (§5.5) is a finance reading of “the model can name the right consideration but does not let it govern the output.” Read through Anderson’s ACT* lens [25], this is a declarative →procedural deficit: the agent holds the declarative knowledge (it states the correct consideration — “VIX is elevated, reduce momentum exposure”) but lacks the compiled procedural production rule that would convert that knowledge into the specific weights — exactly the failure ACT* predicts for knowledge acquired from text without compilation through practice. On this reading the coherence gap, near-invariant across backtest and live (+0.33 / +0.34), is — as the most parsimonious interpretation we offer, not a tested causal claim — consistent with the deficit rather than measurement noise: the declarative store transfers cleanly to new data (the agent still names the right factors) while the missing procedural compilation transfers too (the actions still do not follow), so the gap reproduces at the same magnitude on data the models never saw. The reflect-and-learn loop (the ALE stage and three-tier memory, §3.6) is then the mechanism by which a benchmark could, in principle, supply the practice ACT* says is required — making the memory ablation (§7.7) a direct test of whether in-context experience can begin to compile the missing rules. Acuity is salience attention under polluted context —the negative-Acuity autonomous run is a finance reading of distractibility amid abundant, partly misleading data. Composure is robustness to perturbation vs. overcorrection —gemini-autonomous’s vol-driven churn (§7.6) is reactive overcorrection. Discipline is 

systematic alignment to a standing mandate vs. turn-by-turn instruction-following —self-regulation without a hard guardrail. Reliability is protocol/format adherence and operational robustness — completing a structured tool-use task in valid form, every time. Read this way, the scorecard is a portable lens: the same five questions can be asked of any agentic system operating a complex, cost-bearing, partially observable process. Parallels in the benchmark literature. Three of CLQT’s findings rhyme with results obtained by very different instruments, which is mild evidence they are properties of the 

agents , not of our setup. First, the coherence gap echoes Profit Mirage [8]: where they show apparent alpha collapses once leakage is removed , we show apparent skill fails to cohere with stated reasoning — both are cases of a headline number evaporating under a 40 closer look, and both argue for measuring the process rather than trusting the outcome. Second, the capability ̸ = outcome result (the Sharpe “winner” is a 5-round artifact, §7.3) is the portfolio-management instance of procedure-aware evaluation’s “corrupt success” [12] — task success reached through an unsound process — which CLQT surfaces because the audit trail scores the process, not just the PnL. Third, the repeated-run noise floor (§7.7) is a concrete instance of the statistical-power cautions that agent-evaluation surveys raise in the abstract: a single-axis, single-run score is not just incomplete but unstable ,which is why CLQT reports a profile, bounds module-importance with a repeated-run protocol, and treats reliability as a first-class axis. 

8.3 Scaffolding × Capability, and the Cost of Intelligence 

The structured ↔autonomous contrast is the clearest interaction in the campaign: struc-ture lifts the capability profile broadly (Composure, Discipline) — most dramatically for deepseek (APM-CS +36) — but it also demands a governance schema that stresses the smaller models in different ways (gemini’s genuine parse-fails; qwen3’s operational truncation + rate-limiting, §7.5). Structure thus helps and hurts the same model on different axes — and, for deepseek, reverses with mode (reliable structured, less reliable autonomous) — a more useful statement than “scaffolding helps weaker models,” and one only a multi-axis read can make. The efficiency ledger sharpens it: gemini ·structured is by far the heaviest reasoner (~275k reasoning tokens/run despite thinking being pinned off), its compute consumed by the retry loop around its schema failures. “Cost of intelligence” here is dominated by failed reasoning, not productive reasoning (Figure 14). Which investment schema — structured, autonomous, or hybrid? The live week sharpens the structured ↔autonomous contrast into a design choice. Structure acts as a containment 

layer: it converts a model’s raw fragility into graceful degradation (minimax-m3 keeps trading in structured mode but collapses to holds when autonomous, §7.11) and lifts Composure and Discipline broadly. Autonomy removes that scaffolding — which penalizes 

models that cannot self-govern (gemini-3.5-flash, minimax-m3) yet rewards the more capable ones: claude-sonnet-4.6 scores higher autonomous than structured, and deepseek-v4-pro scores equally in both modes. The implication is that the right schema is model-dependent, and the interesting frontier is neither pole but a hybrid — let an agent operate autonomously while a lightweight governance layer (schema validation, mandate reconciliation, a held-out coherence check) catches exactly the failure modes structure would have prevented, keeping the upside of autonomy without its brittleness. Whether an LLM is best deployed as a disciplined expert-human proxy (structured) or trusted to 

exceed human heuristics (autonomous) is therefore not a single answer but a function of the model’s self-governance capability — which the scorecard measures directly. We pose this as an open, evidence-informed question rather than a prescription; settling it is a natural use of the instrument as the model panel and the live horizon grow.

The cost-tier ablation surfaced its own measurement bug: the HIGH (Almgren–Chriss) tier charges an implausible ~+527 bps/round, so it is mis-calibrated, not “pessimistic.” We therefore keep LOW (calibrated against the live broker paper fills, §5.2) and treat HIGH-tier recalibration as future work — an example of the instrument auditing itself . Because every decision artifact is persisted and hash-chained, CLQT supports a qualita-tive, reproducible read of how models reason and behave (method §6.5; results §7.11) — intent-vs-clip gaps, Risk-Officer negotiations, tool-foraging order, and lesson forma-tion/reuse, each anecdote resolving to a hash-verified round. We frame this as behavior plus self-report, not hidden chain-of-thought, complementary to the axes: the axes quantify where a configuration fails; the behavioral read explains, in the model’s own words and actions, how . We regard it as a primary use of the audit trail, not a side effect. 

8.7 Limitations 

We read this single-period campaign through the standard threats-to-validity frame: what the design can identify, what its constructs assume, and the conditions under which its conclusions hold. 

• What returns can identify. By construction the returns axis is underpowered to attribute module value at this scale: every single-module and cluster knockout lands inside the ±0.42 repeated-run band (§7.7), so no module-importance claim can rest on returns — we read the flat ∆Sharpe as a statement about the metric, not the modules, and confine module value to the capability axes (where, e.g., analysis-blind drives Acuity to 0.02). Two related scope conditions: the HIGH cost tier is mis-calibrated (~527 bps/round; LOW retained), and Acuity and Discipline are mode-confounded, so they are read only within mode. 

• Construct validity of the axes. The five axes operationalize latent capabilities, and the v2 hardening of Discipline and Composure shows the read is sensitive to how those constructs are defined. We argue construct validity empirically rather than assume it — rank-stability across the revision ( τ = 0.78, ρ = 0.90), near-orthogonality (mean |r| = 0.28), and robustness to leave-one-out and judge-reweighting (Appendix F) — and note candidly that the revision overturned one prior sub-finding (memory “breaking” Composure was an artifact of the earlier construction). 

• Judge independence. A held-out, non-cohort judge mitigates but cannot fully eliminate LLM-as-judge self-preference. A second, different-family judge corrob-orates the headline gap (GLM-5.2, ρ = 0.65; +0.28/+0.34 vs +0.33/+0.34), yet minimax-m3 also sits in the live cohort, so its own live rounds are self-adjudicated — it rates itself among the lowest (no inflation), but a fully clean design would draw the judge from outside both panels (Appendix C, Table C1). 

• External validity: one regime, modeled backtest costs. The window is a single, predominantly rising market (SPY +27%, max DD −8.9%, VIX median 17), and backtest costs are modeled rather than executed (the live track supplies real fills on the daily deltas). This bounds every returns-based statement to one regime, and the bound cuts both ways: the benign window masks the modules whose value concentrates at turning points and flatters high-exposure risk-taking while penalizing the prudent de-risking the agents exhibit (§7.7). The capability and risk-adjusted axes are comparatively regime-robust, but absolute-return conclusions should be read as regime-conditional. 43 • Live evidence: consistency, not transfer. The live track shows the instrument measures the same decision property on genuinely unseen data, not that any specific model generalizes — the live and backtest cohorts are different model versions, and live returns are indicative-only by design (daily flatten; overnight exposure is virtual and modeled). The short horizon keeps Acuity and Composure the shortest-baseline axes; tellingly, the second live week overturned two week-1 reads and converted an apparent day-2 hold “collapse” into an intermittent, recoverable rate (§7.11) — itself a caution against over-reading any short live record, ours included. None of these undercut the contribution — the instrument and its read-out — but all bound what the numbers may claim. 

8.8 Future Work 

The instrument is built to be populated over time; the priorities are the dimensions along which this paper’s reading is thinnest, not a longer list of run parameters. 

• Scale and statistical power. Broaden the model panel and the number of repeated runs so every axis carries confidence intervals and rank-stability rather than point estimates and run-ranges — turning the current map into one with quantified uncertainty. 

• Regime robustness. This campaign sat in a single benign, rising market. Strati-fying rounds by market regime and reporting how far each capability degrades from calm into stress is the load-bearing extension before any returns-based conclusion can generalize. 

• Generalization of the profile. Varying the standing mandate and the reasoning-effort budget tests whether a model’s capability fingerprint is a stable property or an artifact of the single mandate and effort level used here. 

• Active stress and instrument hardening. Adversarial misleading-data and canary probes would stress Acuity and Composure directly rather than relying on naturally-occurring noise; a broader held-out judge panel, a recalibrated HIGH cost tier, and a judge drawn from outside the live cohort would further harden the measurement itself. Finer-grained per-sector and per-name attribution is a natural next layer, letting the same audit trail localize not only where a process fails but on which exposures. 

## 9. Conclusion 

CLQT is a closed-loop, cost-aware, temporally-gated benchmark whose purpose is diagnostic: its tamper-evident audit trail and five-axis capability scorecard characterize how LLM agents reason and behave in autonomous portfolio management — surfacing competencies, limitations, and unanticipated behaviors alike — rather than ranking them by a single quarter’s return. On a chain-verified year-long bi-weekly campaign (5 models ×

2 modes, repeated runs for the reliable models, a 13-configuration ablation grid, a held-out judge, and a live broker track) the instrument cleanly separates outcome from capability — the capability leader (deepseek ·structured) is not the nominal Sharpe winner (exposed as 44 a 5-of-26-round qwen3 artifact), and no configuration dominates all five axes — surfaces model-specific limitations (structured governance-schema adherence) invisible to returns, exposes mode-specific stability characteristics (volatility-driven churn) and a cohort-wide coherence weakness an independent judge reveals, and even audits its own cost model. Adding a two-week live broker paper-trading track (nine valid days), we show the instrument transfers to genuinely unseen data: the signal–action-agreement ↔judge coherence gap is essentially the same on backtest and live (+0.33 / +0.34), and the live track exposes an autonomous-only hold behavior — an intermittent, recoverable, per-model hold-rate decomposing into two distinct model-specific mechanisms (tool-turn exhaustion vs schema-adherence failure) the structured scaffold prevents. We deliberately decline to rank: a credible ranking needs an order of magnitude more models and regimes than any single study affords, and the lasting contribution of a benchmark is the reusable map of limitations it produces. Broader repeated-run, regime-stratified campaigns can populate that map further; this paper establishes the instrument that draws it.

## Appendix E: Behavioral Anecdotes (round-id-pinned) 

Representative behaviors from the decision trail, each resolvable to a hash-verified round (the full bank is available from the authors on request). Read neutrally — the same audit field that exposes a failure mode credits a competence. Module-detach collapses the decision process (the behavioral diagnostic in §7.7). On one round (2025-11-03), same anchor model: the full agent weighs ~128 signals, scouts ~35 candidates, and commits a mean-variance-optimal book with explicit weights, an exclusion list, binding constraints, and a cost-management lesson; the bare-workflow 

agent weighs ~56 signals, scouts none, and its entire rationale is a one-line note accepting a default risk-parity book with no constraints, no adjustments, and an empty reflection — premature construction, presumptuous intent, absent scrutiny, yet the caps keep the realized book safe, so returns are unaffected (Table 9). Two roads to a hold (autonomous mode). gemini-3.5-flash (2026-06-16) holds a full mean-variance thesis in its reflection, but its committed round records only a truncation marker — it exhausted its tool-turn budget exploring and never serialized a final allocation. 

minimax-m3 (2026-06-16) emits schema-invalid output (an empty value where a list was required); the round is rejected and falls back to hold. Competent, signal-grounded decisions. claude-sonnet-4.6 (2026-06-15) reads a risk-on regime, flags a negative momentum loading in its factor model, and rotates to correct it, exiting its worst-momentum and highest-volatility names. deepseek-v4-pro (2026-06-17) rotates from deteriorating mega-cap tech into cyclicals and financials, citing a large-cap sentiment collapse against improving trajectories elsewhere. gpt-5.4-mini (2026-06-17) tilts to the strongest-momentum names, keeps an overweight where the news trend improved, and trims negative-momentum or deteriorating-sentiment ones. These illustrate the two hold mechanisms (§7.11) alongside the signal-grounded reasoning the scorecard rewards (claude-sonnet-4.6 Coherence 0.57, APM-CS 69.1). 

## Appendix F: Capability-Axis Robustness (construct validity) 

This version hardens two axes (§6.4) and validates that the change does not rewrite the conclusions. Discipline (D4) is now bounded, 12 self_constrained + 12 (1 −clip_dependence )

— the earlier draft scored only the pre-MAPC self-constraint rate plus a turnover-cap leg that was a no-op at campaign turnover (~0.15). Composure (D3) is now the mean of its legs rather than their product, which let one weak leg zero the axis and (since the consistency leg comes from the memory module) made Composure uncomputable under the memory ablation — the mean falls back to the memory-free leg. All numbers come from scripts/analyze_axis_robustness.py over the per-run scorecard. F.1 — Rank stability (v1 → v2). Re-scoring the cohort under the old construction and the new one and comparing the APM-CS orderings gives Kendall τ = 0.78 (p < 0.001) 49 and Spearman ρ = 0.90 across the ten configurations: the main claims are unchanged — deepseek ·structured is the APM-CS leader and qwen3 ·structured is the Reliability artifact under both constructions. The only movement is in the two configurations v1 Discipline over-credited (a perfect self-constraint rate without the clip-dependence penalty) — the fix behaving as intended. The revision overturned exactly one prior sub-finding — that removing memory “breaks” Composure — an artifact of the old uncomputable construction; under v2, memory removal merely drops the consistency leg (§7.7). F.2 — Orthogonality (non-redundancy). Across the ten off-diagonal axis pairs the mean |Pearson r| is 0.28 (max 0.58, Coherence ↔Discipline; Figure F1), so the five axes are near-orthogonal — they measure distinct properties rather than restating one underlying score. The strongest pair (Coherence vs. Discipline, r ≈ − 0.58) is itself interpretable: configurations that self-constrain hardest are not the ones whose allocations best follow their stated reasoning.  

> Figure F1. Inter-axis Pearson correlation across the campaign cohort (D1–D5). Off-diagonal magnitudes are small (mean 0.28), evidence the five axes are non-redundant.

F.3 — Weight sensitivity. Re-aggregating APM-CS under leave-one-axis-out (five variants) and under reweighting the two Coherence components (agreement vs. judge from 0.3/0.7 to 0.7/0.3) leaves the configuration ordering stable: Kendall τ ≥ 0.64 against the equal-weight baseline in every case (the Coherence-reweighting variants stay at τ ≥ 0.91).
